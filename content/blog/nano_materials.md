# Nanomaterials in Food Technology

Nanotechnology in the food industry involves the use of materials at the nanoscale (typically 1 to 1000 nanometers) to improve food safety, packaging, and nutritional quality. Because these particles are so small, they exhibit entirely different physical and chemical properties compared to their macro-scale counterparts.

## 1. Nanomaterials as Food Components

Nanomaterials can be introduced directly into food, either naturally or through engineering, to enhance the product.

* **Naturally Occurring Nanomaterials:** Many standard foods already contain nano-sized components. For example, DNA is about 2.5 nm wide. Milk naturally contains nano-sized casein protein micelles and whey proteins, which help the body absorb nutrients efficiently.


* **Nano-encapsulation (Engineered):** This is a critical biotechnology process where bioactive ingredients (like vitamins or antioxidants) are enclosed in a protective nanoscale shell.


* **Benefits:** It masks unpleasant tastes/aromas, protects the nutrient from degrading in stomach acid, and greatly enhances the solubility and bioavailability of water-insoluble supplements.





## 2. Nanomaterials in Food Packaging

The most widespread use of nanotechnology in the food sector is in packaging. The industry is moving away from non-biodegradable petroleum-based plastics toward "green" nanocomposites.

* **Active Packaging:** Nanoparticles act as physical barriers, heavily restricting the passage of oxygen and moisture (steam), which delays food spoilage.


* **Smart/Intelligent Packaging (Nanosensors):** Nanosensors can detect food spoilage by reacting to small changes in food color or the gases produced by bacteria.


* *Example:* Gold-based nanoparticles are used to rapidly detect the presence of Aflatoxin B1 (a dangerous fungal toxin) in milk.

## 3. Classification of Nanomaterials

### Metallic Nanomaterials

Metallic and metal-oxide nanoparticles are primarily used for their highly effective **antimicrobial and antifungal properties**.

* **Examples:** Silver (Ag), Copper (Cu), Zinc Oxide (ZnO), and Titanium Dioxide ($TiO_2$).
* **Mechanism:** They release ions that disrupt the cell walls of spoiling bacteria, preventing them from multiplying.
* **Safety Concern:** The size of these particles must be strictly controlled. For instance, $TiO_2$ nanoparticles must be kept between 110–300 nm to ensure they do not cause harmful damage to human cells.



### Carbon Nanomaterials

Carbon-based nanomaterials are popular because they can often be synthesized through eco-friendly "green routes" and do not contaminate the food.

* **Examples:** Graphene, Carbon nanotubes (CNTs), and Carbon dots.
* **Application:** They provide immense mechanical strength to packaging and antibacterial protection.
* **Industry Use:** Adding graphene to polylactic acid (a biodegradable plastic) creates a highly stable, oxygen-resistant wrapper that significantly increases the shelf life of snacks like potato chips.



### Organic Nanomaterials

These are derived from natural, edible biopolymers. They are the safest option because they are completely non-toxic and biodegradable.

* **Examples:** Chitosan (derived from crustacean shells), Starch, Gelatin, and Agar.
* **Application:** Used to create edible films and coatings directly on the food.
* **Drawback:** Organic materials like chitosan have low moisture resistance. To fix this, they are often reinforced with metallic or silica nanoparticles to form a stronger "nanocomposite film".



### Silicon Nanomaterials

Silicon-based nanomaterials are widely used for thermal stability and moisture control.

* **Examples:** Silicon dioxide and Silica aerogels.
* **Application:** Silicon dioxide is directly used as a food colorant, a drying agent, and an anti-caking agent to prevent powders from clumping. Silica aerogels are incorporated into packaging (like polyvinyl alcohol films) to provide exceptional thermal stability, making them perfect for temperature-sensitive items like chocolate.
* **Safety Concern:** Like metallic nanoparticles, excessive ingestion or improper sizing of silica nanoparticles can be toxic to human cells.
