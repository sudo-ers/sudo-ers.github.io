# Type I Nutritional Disorders

Nutritional disorders occur when a person's dietary intake lacks the right amount of nutrients (undernutrition), contains an excess of specific nutrients (overnutrition), or has an incorrect balance. In your syllabus, Type I disorders are broadly grouped into two main categories: **Lifestyle-related overnutrition diseases** and **Severe undernutrition diseases**.

## 1. Causes of Lifestyle Diseases

Lifestyle diseases are chronic, non-communicable diseases whose occurrence is primarily based on the daily habits of individuals and their relationship with their environment.

* **Primary Causes:** Lack of physical activity, poor and unbalanced nutrition, excessive alcohol consumption, and tobacco use.
* **Dietary Triggers:** High consumption of saturated fats, trans fats, highly refined carbohydrates, and sodium-rich processed foods.
* **Physiological Impact:** These habits lead to metabolic disturbances, culminating in conditions like obesity, hypertension, and cardiovascular issues.

> [!NOTE]
> **Exam Strategy:** If a 13-mark question asks you to "Discuss Type I Nutritional Disorders," always start by clearly separating the disorders into two distinct headings: **Lifestyle/Overnutrition** (CVD, Hypertension, Obesity) and **Undernutrition/Deficiency** (Kwashiorkor, Marasmus). This shows the examiner a clear logical flow.

### Lifestyle & Overnutrition Disorders

## 2. Cardiovascular Diseases (CVD)

Cardiovascular diseases are conditions affecting the heart and blood vessels, primarily driven by dietary choices that damage the arterial walls.

* **Atherosclerosis:** This is the underlying mechanism of most heart diseases. Saturated fats and trans fats cause fatty deposits (plaque) to build up on the inside of artery walls, narrowing the space for blood flow.
* **Role of Processed Meats:** Foods preserved with salts and nitrites (like sausages and deli meats) significantly increase the risk of heart problems due to chemical damage and sodium overload.
* **Role of Refined Carbs:** Highly processed grains (white bread, white rice) and sugary sweets cause severe blood sugar spikes. This triggers the liver to produce excess fat, compounding the risk for both heart disease and diabetes.

## 3. Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

Hypertension occurs when the force of blood pushing against the artery walls is consistently too high, forcing the heart to work harder.

* **Dietary Causes:** High sodium intake and heavy alcohol consumption (more than two drinks a day) are major risk factors.
* **Dietary Prevention:** Plant-based diets (vegetarian and particularly vegan) are strongly associated with lower blood pressure compared to meat-heavy diets.
* **Nutrient-Drug Interaction:** Patients treated with thiazide diuretics for hypertension often experience severe potassium loss. They must compensate by eating potassium-rich foods like bananas, citrus fruits, and potatoes.

## 4. Obesity

Obesity is a chronic, recurring complex disorder characterized by an excessive accumulation of body fat that impairs health.

* **Multifactorial Causes:** It is not just about overeating; obesity is influenced by a complex combination of genetics, hormonal imbalances, behavior, and environmental factors.
* **Health Risks:** It drastically increases the risk for Type 2 diabetes, hypertension, coronary heart disease, and certain types of cancer.
* **Management:** Treatment involves increasing physical activity, reducing caloric intake, and in severe cases, bariatric surgery.

> [!TIP]
> **Mandatory Point:** When writing about obesity management, always include this specific metric from your syllabus: *"Losing as little as 5% to 10% of total body weight can significantly lessen weight-related metabolic problems."* ---

### Severe Undernutrition Disorders

## 5. Kwashiorkor (Protein Deficiency)

Kwashiorkor is a severe form of malnutrition caused almost entirely by a **lack of protein** in the diet, even if overall caloric intake is somewhat adequate (usually from a high-carb, low-protein diet).

* **Visual Symptoms:** Patients exhibit an extremely emaciated appearance in their limbs.
* **Key Distinguishing Feature:** Despite the muscle wasting, patients develop severe **edema** (fluid retention), causing their belly, ankles, and feet to swell massively.

## 6. Marasmus (Overall Caloric Deficiency)

Unlike Kwashiorkor, Marasmus is a severe deficiency of **all macronutrients**—carbohydrates, fats, and proteins.

* **Physiological Impact:** The body completely lacks the fuel necessary to maintain normal body functions, leading to total calorie insufficiency.
* **Visual Symptoms:** Patients are visibly depleted, severely underweight, and lack any subcutaneous fat or muscle tissue.
* **Diagnostic Metric:** A child with marasmus usually has a weight-for-height value that is more than **3 standard deviations** below the healthy average for their age and gender.
