Microbial polysaccharides are high-molecular-weight carbohydrates produced by microorganisms like bacteria, fungi, yeast, and algae. These biopolymers generally function as water-soluble gums and possess novel, highly unique physicochemical properties.

## Classification

Microbial polysaccharides are classified based on where they are produced:

* **Intracellular Polysaccharides:** Produced and stored inside the cell.
	* Acts as a carbon and energy reserve.
	* Example: Glycogen.


* **Extracellular Polysaccharides (EPS):** Secreted outside the cell into the environment.
	* Examples: Xanthan, Gellan.

```
               Microbial Polysaccharides
                           |
          +----------------+----------------+
          |                                 |
   Intracellular                    Extracellular (EPS)
          |                                 |
 Produced and stored                Secreted outside the cell 
 inside the cell                    into the environment.
          |                                 |
 Example: Glycogen                  Examples: Xanthan, Gellan
 (Acts as carbon/energy reserve)    (Creates protective capsules)
```

> [!TIP]
> The syllabus text mentions "glycogen" as an intracellular reserve. However, the polysaccharides actually used in the food industry as thickeners and gelling agents are almost exclusively Extracellular Polysaccharides (EPS). Pointing this out in your exam shows a deeper understanding.

## Key Characteristics and Advantages

Microbial polysaccharides are industrially important substances, competing with traditional natural gums derived from marine algae or higher plants.

Their superior properties include:

* **High Stability:** They remain highly active and stable at extreme temperatures, pH levels, and salinity.
* **Safety & Sustainability:** They are completely nontoxic, biodegradable, and environmentally friendly.
* **Rheological Superiority:** They possess unique flow and deformation properties due to their high purity and regular molecular structures.
* **Cost-Effectiveness:** They can be produced at a lower cost than extracting gums from plants.

## Major Applications in the Food Industry

Due to their diverse structures and properties, these biopolymers have a wide range of industrial applications. They are primarily used as:

* **Emulsifiers:** To keep oil and water mixed.
* **Stabilizers:** To maintain the structure of food over time.
* **Gelling Agents:** To give foods a jelly-like consistency.
* **Thickeners & Suspending Agents:** To improve the viscosity of liquids.
* **Binders & Coagulants:** To hold ingredients together.

> [!NOTE]
> A common Part B question is "Discuss the applications of microbial polysaccharides in the food industry as alternatives to plant gums." To get full marks, do not just list the applications from the text. Provide specific microbial examples to prove you understand the biotechnology aspect.

### Essential Examples to Memorize

To make your answer comprehensive, include these standard food industry examples:

* **Xanthan Gum:** Produced by the bacterium *Xanthomonas campestris*. Used extensively as a thickener and stabilizer in dressings and sauces.
* **Gellan Gum:** Produced by the bacterium *Sphingomonas elodea*. Used as a highly effective gelling agent, especially in vegan dairy alternatives.
* **Pullulan:** Produced by the fungus *Aureobasidium pullulans*. Used to make edible films and as a low-calorie food binder.

## Future Scope

Employing genetically modified microorganisms under controlled fermentation conditions can result in the production of new exopolysaccharides. These novel biopolymers will possess tailor-made, superior properties that will open up entirely new areas of industrial food applications.
