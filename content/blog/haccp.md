# Definition of HACCP

**Hazard Analysis Critical Control Point (HACCP)** is a science-based, systematic food safety management system. It addresses food safety through the analysis and control of biological, chemical, and physical hazards. Instead of relying mainly on end-product testing, HACCP focuses on identifying potential problems and preventing them before they occur.

# HACCP Principles (Based on Text)

While formal HACCP has multiple steps, the provided text highlights the following core principles:

- **Hazard Analysis:** The most important principle. It identifies the specific biological, chemical, or physical hazards that could occur at each step of the manufacturing process.

- **Critical Control Points (CCP):** Points in the process where potential problems are critical to food safety. At a CCP, a hazard can be prevented, eliminated, or reduced to acceptable levels.

    - _Examples:_ Thermal processing, chilling, testing for chemical residues, formulation control, and metal contaminant testing.

- **Monitoring:** Essential to confirm that critical limits are being met. This consists of manual or automatic measurements and observations at each CCP.

# Microbiological Criteria

A **microbiological criterion** provides guidance on the acceptability of a foodstuff or its manufacturing process. It defines whether a product or food lot is acceptable based on the absence, presence, or number of microorganisms (including parasites) and their toxins/metabolites per unit of measurement.

**A complete criterion must include:**

- The microorganisms/toxins of concern and why they are a concern.

- Analytical methods for detection/quantification.

- A sampling plan (number of field samples and size of the analytical unit).

- Appropriate microbiological limits.

- The number of units that must conform to these limits.

- The specific food and point in the food chain it applies to.

- Actions to take if the criterion is not met.

# Purpose of Microbiological Criteria

To make the best use of money and manpower, criteria should only be applied where they offer maximum benefit in ensuring food safety.

- **For Regulatory Authorities:** Used to define and check compliance with food safety requirements.

- **For Food Business Operators:** Used to formulate design requirements and test end-products to verify and validate that their HACCP plan is working effectively.

# Types / Aspects of Microbiological Criteria

The text identifies three main components or aspects evaluated in criteria:

- **Microorganisms/Toxins:** Includes bacteria, viruses, yeasts, moulds, algae, parasitic protozoa, helminths, and their toxins/metabolites.

- **Microbiological Methods:** For perishable foods, methods must yield results before the food is consumed or expires. They must be reasonable in terms of complexity, equipment availability, ease of interpretation, time, and cost.

- **Microbiological Limits:** Must be based on data appropriate to the food, gathered from establishments already operating under Good Hygienic Practices and HACCP.

# Importance of HACCP

- It provides a comprehensive framework for monitoring the _total_ food system from harvesting to consumption.

- It is specifically designed to significantly reduce the risk of foodborne illnesses.

- It ensures safety is actively managed at every step of production.

# Advantages of HACCP

- It is science-based and systematic. 
- Its primary advantage is that it is proactive - focusing heavily on prevention and controlling potential problems before they occur.
